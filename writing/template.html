In evaluating whether people's attitudes are shaped more by their immediate surroundings than by society as a whole, one must consider both psychological behavior and real-world examples. While societal norms certainly have a role in shaping long-term beliefs and values, I agree with the statement that people's attitudes are primarily determined by their immediate situation or environment, as it influences their emotional and behavioral responses more directly and immediately.

To begin with, individuals tend to adjust their attitudes based on the context they are in at any given time. A powerful example can be seen in military contexts. A person raised in a peaceful society may hold strong anti-violence beliefs, yet when placed in the immediate environment of a war zone, their attitude toward violence and authority may change rapidly. The emotional and survival pressures in such situations override general societal values. This shows how powerful local, immediate situations can be in shaping a person's stance and behavior.

Similarly, consider the academic environment in schools. A student may be raised in a culture that praises education, but if they find themselves in a classroom with little support or among peers who belittle academic achievement, their attitude toward learning might shift. This change is not due to a transformation in societal values but rather the more immediate influence of surroundings such as peer pressure, teacher behavior, and school culture. This demonstrates how daily settings can influence even attitudes we assume are grounded in larger cultural values.

This makes it clear that immediate surroundings are more decisive in determining attitudes.
